Worksheet 03

Crafting a Research Question

Move from a broad topic to a focused research question. A strong research question is specific, interpretive, and evidence-based — this worksheet will help you get there.

1 Your Topic

What broad topic or theme interests you? This can be general — we'll narrow it down.

2 Your Primary Source

What source are you working with? Identify the specific text, object, or material you'll be analyzing.

3 An Interesting Observation

What moment in your source do you find interesting, surprising, or confusing? Describe it. This is often where a good research question begins — with something that makes you pause.

4 Draft Your Research Question

Write a first attempt at a research question. Don't worry about getting it perfect — you'll revise it after the checklist below.

5 Evaluate Your Question

Use this checklist to test your research question against the three key criteria. Check each box once you're satisfied, and use the notes field to reflect on how you might strengthen that aspect.

Is it specific?
A strong research question is narrowly tailored to a particular theme, moment, or interest — not a broad survey of an entire topic.
Is it interpretive?
It should not be answerable with yes, no, or maybe. Frame it through "how" or "why" to invite analysis rather than a simple factual answer.
Is it evidence-based?
Your question should reference or be grounded in at least one source. It should be answerable through close analysis of evidence, not opinion alone.
6 Revised Research Question

Based on the checklist above, revise your research question. Aim for something specific enough to guide your research, interpretive enough to sustain an argument, and grounded in evidence.
