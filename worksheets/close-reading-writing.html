Worksheet 02

Close Reading for Writing

Turn your observations into written analysis. This worksheet guides you through building a paragraph that lets evidence drive the argument — rather than using evidence as proof of a pre-existing claim.

1 Your Evidence

What passage are you writing about? Paste the specific quotation or describe the moment you're analyzing.

2 Your Observations

What specifically do you notice about the language, imagery, structure, or tone? Be as detailed and concrete as possible — these observations will become the engine of your analysis.

3 Unpack the Evidence

Rather than using the evidence as "proof" of a pre-existing claim, dig into what the evidence itself reveals. What does this passage show us when you sit with it? What meaning emerges from close attention?

4 Connections and Patterns

How does this moment connect to other parts of the text? Does it complicate, support, or shift your reading? Think about how this passage fits into the larger work.

5 Draft Your Analysis

Write a paragraph that starts with the evidence and lets your observations drive the analysis. Lead with what you found — not with what you're trying to prove. Let the close reading do the work.
