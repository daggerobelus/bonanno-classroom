Worksheet 03

Writing an Annotated Bibliography Entry

Practice the two-move structure for a strong annotation: summarize your source's argument, then connect it to your research project.

1 MLA Citation

Write the full MLA citation for your source. Include author, title, publication information, and date. This will appear at the top of your annotated bibliography entry.

2 Summarize the Source

In your own words, summarize the source's main argument or purpose. What is the author trying to say? What evidence do they use? Aim for 2–3 sentences.

3 Assess the Source

Evaluate your source using the criteria below. Check each box once you've considered it, and use the notes field to capture your thinking.

Is this source reliable?
Consider the author's credentials, the publication venue, and whether the source uses evidence to support its claims.
How does it compare to other sources?
Does this source agree with, complicate, or contradict other sources you've found? How does it fit into the larger conversation?
What are its limitations?
Does the source have gaps, biases, or a narrow scope? What doesn't it address that might be relevant to your project?
4 Reflect — Connect to Your Project

How does this source connect to your research project? Does it provide evidence for your argument, offer context, or present a counterpoint? Explain why you chose this source and how you plan to use it.

5 Draft the Full Annotation

Using your work above, draft the full annotation for this source. Aim for 5–10 sentences that summarize the source, assess its reliability, and reflect on its connection to your project.
