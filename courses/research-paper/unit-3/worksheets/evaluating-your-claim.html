Worksheet 02

Paragraph-Level Argumentation

Build a single body paragraph from the ground up — developing a subclaim, selecting evidence, and writing the reasoning that connects them. Use this worksheet as many times as you need, once for each paragraph.

1 Your Main Claim

Write your current main claim here for reference. Each body paragraph should support a different piece of this argument — so keep it visible as you work.

2 What's Your Entry Point?

For this paragraph, are you starting with a subclaim you want to make, a piece of evidence you want to work with, or something else? There's no single right way in. Write down what you're starting from.

3 Subclaim

A subclaim is the argument this paragraph makes — a smaller, more focused piece of your main claim. It's not a topic sentence that announces what you'll discuss; it's a contestable statement that this paragraph will work to support. What is this paragraph arguing?

4 Evidence

Evidence is the source material that supports your subclaim — quotations, data, specific details from your sources. Choose evidence that does real work for your argument. A good check: if you can remove a source and your analysis stays the same, you might not be engaging deeply enough with it.

5 Reasoning

Reasoning is where you explain how and why your evidence supports your subclaim. This is the part that's hardest to write — and usually the part that needs the most space. Don't assume the connection is obvious to your reader. A reader may accept your evidence but still doubt that it means what you say it means.

6 Draft the Paragraph

Using the subclaim, evidence, and reasoning you've developed above, draft this body paragraph. It doesn't need to be polished — the goal is to get the argument on the page so you can see how the pieces work together.

7 Check the Connection

Read your paragraph back. Does the subclaim clearly connect to your main claim? Does the evidence do work that's specific to this subclaim, or could it just as easily support a different argument? Is there enough reasoning, or are you leaving the reader to make the leap on their own?
