Worksheet 01

Building Your Main Claim

Develop, evaluate, and strengthen the thesis statement for your research paper — the main argument that will drive everything else.

1 Where Are You Starting?

A main claim doesn't always come first — sometimes you start with a piece of evidence that fascinates you, a question you can't shake, or a hunch about a text. Where are you right now in your thinking? Write down whatever you have so far, in whatever shape it's in.

2 Draft a Main Claim

Your main claim is the thesis statement of your paper — the argument you'll state in your introduction and support throughout. It can be more than one sentence. Using whatever you wrote above as a starting point, try drafting a main claim now. Don't worry about getting it perfect — you'll revise it by the end of this worksheet.

3 Test Your Claim

A strong main claim is specific, contestable, and significant. Use this checklist to evaluate what you have so far. Check each box once you're satisfied, and use the notes to think through how you might strengthen that aspect.

Is it specific?
Is the claim based in evidence? Does it unpack key terms? Does it explain the relationship between ideas? Does it answer questions of "how" and "why?"
Is it contestable?
Is the claim something that a knowledgeable reader could reasonably doubt? Is it merely an observation about something factual or obvious, or does it make an argument?
Is it significant?
Does the claim answer the question of "so what?" Why does this claim matter to a knowledgeable reader? How does it help us understand something new, different, or nonobvious about the topic?
4 Watch Out for Common Pitfalls

Sometimes claims look like they meet the criteria but fall into common traps. Read through the descriptions below and consider whether your claim might be one of these. Check any that apply.

Five-Paragraph Essay Claim
The claim makes interpretive moves but doesn't offer something nonobvious. Its structure suggests one paragraph per example, each proving the same point. Try: choosing a more specific passage, complicating the claim, or defining key terms more precisely.
Overstated Claim
The claim is general and absolute — it may create a false debate between two options and pick one, ignoring complicating evidence. Watch for language like "obviously," "only," or "has to." Try: adding specificity and thinking about how to account for reader doubt.
Understated Claim
The claim contains interesting observations, but the connection between them isn't explicitly stated — a reader has to guess. Try: spelling out the how/why relationship between your ideas, or expanding the claim to give yourself more space.
Descriptive Claim
The claim describes what happens but doesn't make an argument a reader would doubt. Description is a crucial first step — but you need to move beyond it. Try: asking "so what?" and "yes, and?" or asking "how" and "why" questions about what you've described.
5 Revise Your Main Claim

Based on everything above, draft a revised version of your main claim. Your claim may have shifted from where you started — that's often a sign of productive thinking. Give yourself space: a strong main claim can be more than one sentence.
